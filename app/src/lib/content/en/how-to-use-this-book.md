Welcome to this book. Before we start, please take three minutes to read this page. It tells you **where this book wants to take you, how to read it, and one mindset that will save you a lot of detours**.

This book is for **ordinary people who know their way around a computer but have no background in AI or programming**. If you can send and receive email and use a browser and office software, you can read it just fine. **You don't need to know how to code.** When we reach the chapters where you "build a project hands-on," we'll guide you to direct the AI in plain language, not make you memorize commands.

## 1. Where this book wants to take you

By the time you finish, you'll have four things, going from simple to deep:

| | What you'll learn | Where |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1. Understand it | What a large language model really is, why it can hold a conversation, why it gets things wrong. No more being intimidated by a pile of jargon | Part One |
| 2. Use it | Putting AI to work in daily life, study, and your job: asking the right way, feeding it the right material, not getting led astray | Part Two |
| 3. See clearly | Model / product / API / Agent, RAG, local models. One map to make sense of the whole tool ecosystem | Part Three |
| 4. Build | Using **Claude Code and Codex**, the two mainstream tools, to build your own projects | Parts Four and Five |

There's also Part Six at the end, about **risk and limits**: what you can feed AI, what you absolutely can't, and how to use it without burning money, stepping on landmines, or being careless.

> **Key point:** This isn't an "AI tips and tricks" collection. It wants you to **really understand how AI works**, so you can judge for yourself when to trust it and when you need to check its work. Once you understand the principles, the techniques come naturally.

## 2. Two ways to read, take what you need

You don't have to read it cover to cover. Pick the path that fits you:

- **You just want to use it:** Read **Parts One and Two** (basics + everyday use), then skip to **Part Six** (risk). That's enough to use AI smoothly and steadily.
- **You want to build things:** On top of the above, keep going through **Parts Three, Four, and Five** (tool ecosystem, then coding in practice, then six real projects). Step by step you'll build a personal website, a document tool, a meeting-notes assistant, and even connect AI to your own program.

Whichever path you take, **don't skip Part One**. It's the foundation of the whole book, and every "why use it this way" later is built on it.

## 3. How to use the extras

- **The "Quiz" at the end of each chapter:** Six questions, in four types: concept, misconception, scenario, and hands-on. The answers and explanations are right below each question. Don't just check right or wrong. The point is in the explanations: why an answer is wrong, and who tends to make that mistake.
- **Glossary (Appendix A):** A three-language reference (Chinese, Japanese, English). Look up any word you don't know.
- **Prompt templates (Appendix B):** Fill-in-the-blank templates for everyday, work, study, coding, and other situations.
- **Bilingual (Chinese / Japanese):** This book has a Chinese edition and a Japanese edition. Click "**中文 / 日本語**" at the top of the reader to switch with one tap; the content matches one to one. In the main text, key terms get the English in parentheses **on first use** (for example, context window), so you can look up English material later.
- The reader also supports **jumping through the table of contents on the left, full-text search, and highlighting text and writing notes**. Where you've read and what you've marked are saved automatically.

## 4. One mindset that runs through the whole book

If this book can get you to remember only two sentences, let them be these:

1. **AI is very capable, but it will "be confidently wrong."** What it's after is sounding true, not being true. So **always check important facts, numbers, and quotes yourself**. Treat it as a smart, handy assistant that occasionally talks nonsense, not an encyclopedia that's always right.
2. **By default it "has no memory."** When you see it "remembering you," in almost every case that's **the product layer feeding your information back in each time**, not the model itself actually remembering (chapters 3 and 9 explain this fully). Once you understand this, a lot of confusion about "how did it forget?" and "how does it suddenly remember?" clears up.

> **Key point:** Read every later chapter with these two points in mind ("it will be wrong, it has no memory"), and you'll find that nearly all the book's methods and reminders are teaching you how to live with these two traits and use AI well and steadily.
